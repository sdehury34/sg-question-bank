## Question

A 500 W immersion heater is used to heat 0.50 kg of water. The temperature of the water rises from 20 °C to 40 °C in 84 s. Assuming no heat is lost to the surroundings, calculate the specific heat capacity of water indicated by this experiment.

## Solution

**Step 1 — Calculate the electrical energy supplied.** Power is the rate of energy transfer, so the total energy supplied in time \(t\) is:

$$E = Pt = 500\text{ W} \times 84\text{ s} = 42\,000\text{ J}$$

**Step 2 — Identify the temperature rise.**

$$\Delta\theta = 40\,^\circ\text{C} - 20\,^\circ\text{C} = 20\,^\circ\text{C}$$

**Step 3 — Apply the specific heat capacity equation.** Since no heat is lost, all the electrical energy goes into heating the water: \(E = mc\Delta\theta\), so

$$c = \frac{E}{m\Delta\theta} = \frac{42\,000\text{ J}}{0.50\text{ kg} \times 20\,^\circ\text{C}} = \frac{42\,000}{10} = 4200\text{ J/(kg}\,^\circ\text{C)}$$

**Step 4 — Check.** This closely matches the accepted specific heat capacity of water, 4200 J/(kg °C), which confirms the calculation is consistent (as expected, since the numbers were generated from a genuine heating scenario).

**Common mistake:** using the final temperature (40 °C) or initial temperature (20 °C) alone in place of the temperature *change* \(\Delta\theta\) — the formula requires the rise in temperature (here 20 °C), not either individual reading.

## Answer

The specific heat capacity of water indicated by this experiment is **4200 J/(kg °C)**.
