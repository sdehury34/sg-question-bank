## Question

A ray of light travels inside a block of glass of refractive index 1.52 and strikes the glass-air boundary from inside the glass.

(a) Calculate the critical angle, \(C\), for the glass-air boundary.

(b) The ray strikes the boundary at an angle of incidence of 50° (measured from the normal). State and explain what happens to the ray.

## Solution

**Part (a).**

The critical angle is the angle of incidence (in the denser medium) at which the angle of refraction is exactly 90°. Using Snell's law at the glass-air boundary, going from glass (refractive index \(n\)) to air (refractive index 1):

$$n \sin C = 1 \times \sin 90^\circ = 1 \quad \Rightarrow \quad \sin C = \frac{1}{n}$$

$$\sin C = \frac{1}{1.52} = 0.658$$

$$C = \sin^{-1}(0.658) = 41.1^\circ$$

**Part (b).**

The angle of incidence (50°) is **greater** than the critical angle (41.1°). When the angle of incidence in the denser medium exceeds the critical angle, refraction into the less dense medium can no longer occur — instead, **total internal reflection** takes place.

The ray reflects back into the glass, obeying the law of reflection: it leaves the boundary at 50° to the normal, on the opposite side of the normal from the incident ray, with all of its intensity (no light is transmitted into the air).

**Check.** Since \(50° > C = 41.1°\), the sine of the "would-be" refraction angle would need to satisfy \( \sin(\theta_r) = n \sin(50°) = 1.52 \times 0.766 = 1.164\), which is greater than 1 — an impossible value for a sine, confirming that refraction cannot occur and total internal reflection must happen instead.

**Common mistake:** calculating \(\sin C = n\) instead of \(\sin C = 1/n\) (inverting the ratio), which gives a value greater than 1 and no valid angle at all. Students also sometimes measure the angle of incidence from the surface of the boundary rather than from the normal, which would give a different (and wrong) comparison with the critical angle.

## Answer

(a) The critical angle is \(C = 41.1^\circ\) (3 s.f.).

(b) Since the angle of incidence (50°) exceeds the critical angle (41.1°), **total internal reflection** occurs — the ray reflects entirely back into the glass at 50° to the normal.
