## Question

A spring has a natural (unstretched) length of 12.0 cm. When a load of 4.0 N is hung from it, the spring stretches to a length of 15.6 cm. Assuming the spring obeys Hooke's Law, calculate:

(a) the spring constant \( k \)
(b) the elastic potential energy stored in the spring

## Solution

**(a) Spring constant.** First find the extension:

$$
e = 15.6 - 12.0 = 3.6 \text{ cm} = 0.036 \text{ m}
$$

By Hooke's Law, \( F = ke \), so:

$$
k = \frac{F}{e} = \frac{4.0}{0.036} = 111.111\ldots \approx 111 \text{ N/m (3 s.f.)}
$$

**(b) Elastic potential energy.** The energy stored in a stretched spring is the area under the force–extension graph:

$$
E_p = \frac{1}{2}Fe = \frac{1}{2}(4.0)(0.036) = 0.072 \text{ J}
$$

**Check:** Using the alternative formula \( E_p = \tfrac{1}{2}ke^2 \):

$$
E_p = \tfrac{1}{2}(111.111\ldots)(0.036)^2 = \tfrac{1}{2}(111.111\ldots)(0.001296) = 0.072 \text{ J}
$$

Both methods agree, confirming \( E_p = 0.072 \) J.

**Common mistake:** Using the extended length (15.6 cm) instead of the extension (3.6 cm) in the calculations, or forgetting to convert centimetres to metres before substituting into \( F = ke \).

## Answer

(a) \( k \approx 111 \) N/m
(b) \( E_p = 0.072 \) J
