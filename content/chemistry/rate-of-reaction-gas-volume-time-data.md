## Question

Excess marble chips (calcium carbonate) are added to 50 cm³ of 1.0 mol/dm³ hydrochloric acid. The volume of carbon dioxide gas produced is measured at intervals:

| Time / s | 0 | 20 | 40 | 60 | 80 | 100 |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Volume of CO₂ / cm³ | 0 | 60 | 96 | 114 | 120 | 120 |

1. Calculate the average rate of reaction between \(t = 0\) s and \(t = 20\) s, in cm³/s.
2. Calculate the average rate of reaction between \(t = 40\) s and \(t = 60\) s, in cm³/s.
3. Explain, in terms of particle collisions, why the rate in part 2 is lower than the rate in part 1.

## Solution

**Part 1.** Average rate over an interval is the *change* in volume divided by the *change* in time — not the cumulative volume divided by the time elapsed since the start.

$$\text{rate} = \frac{\Delta V}{\Delta t} = \frac{60 - 0}{20 - 0} = \frac{60}{20} = 3.0\ \text{cm}^3/\text{s}$$

**Part 2.** Using the same method over the interval from \(t = 40\) s to \(t = 60\) s:

$$\text{rate} = \frac{114 - 96}{60 - 40} = \frac{18}{20} = 0.90\ \text{cm}^3/\text{s}$$

**Check.** The total volume rises steeply at first (0 → 60 → 96 cm³ in the first 40 s) and then flattens out (96 → 114 → 120 → 120 cm³), consistent with a reaction that starts fast and slows down before stopping when the acid is used up — so it makes sense that the rate in Part 2 (0.90 cm³/s) is much smaller than in Part 1 (3.0 cm³/s).

**Part 3.** As the reaction proceeds, hydrochloric acid is consumed, so the concentration of H⁺ ions in the solution decreases. With fewer acid particles per unit volume, the frequency of effective collisions between H⁺ ions and the surface of the calcium carbonate decreases, so the rate of reaction decreases.

**Common mistake:** calculating the rate for part 2 as (total volume at 60 s) ÷ (60 s), i.e. \(114 \div 60\), rather than using the *change* in volume over that specific 20 s interval. This gives the average rate from the very start up to \(t=60\) s, not the (slower) rate during the 40–60 s interval itself.

## Answer

1. Average rate (0–20 s) = **3.0 cm³/s**
2. Average rate (40–60 s) = **0.90 cm³/s**
3. The rate falls because acid concentration decreases as the reaction proceeds, lowering the frequency of effective collisions between reacting particles.
