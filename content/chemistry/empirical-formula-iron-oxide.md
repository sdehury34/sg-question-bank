## Question

A sample of an oxide of iron contains 7.0 g of iron and 3.0 g of oxygen. Find the empirical formula of the oxide.

(Relative atomic masses: Fe = 56, O = 16)

## Solution

Find the number of moles of each element:

$$
\text{mol Fe} = \frac{7.0}{56} = 0.125, \qquad \text{mol O} = \frac{3.0}{16} = 0.1875
$$

Divide both by the smaller value (0.125) to get the simplest ratio:

$$
\text{Fe} : \text{O} = \frac{0.125}{0.125} : \frac{0.1875}{0.125} = 1 : 1.5
$$

Since the ratio must be in whole numbers, multiply both sides by 2:

$$
\text{Fe} : \text{O} = 2 : 3
$$

So the empirical formula is \( \text{Fe}_2\text{O}_3 \).

**Check:** The molar mass of \( \text{Fe}_2\text{O}_3 \) is \( 2(56) + 3(16) = 112 + 48 = 160 \). The mass fraction of iron is \( \dfrac{112}{160} = 0.70 \). In a 10.0 g sample this predicts \( 0.70 \times 10.0 = 7.0 \) g of iron and \( 3.0 \) g of oxygen — exactly matching the given data.

**Common mistake:** Stopping at the ratio \( 1 : 1.5 \) and writing the formula as \( \text{FeO}_{1.5} \) instead of scaling up to the smallest whole-number ratio \( 2:3 \).

## Answer

The empirical formula of the oxide is \( \text{Fe}_2\text{O}_3 \).
