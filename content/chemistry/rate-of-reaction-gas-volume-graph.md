## Question

Excess magnesium ribbon was added to 50 cm³ of 2.0 mol/dm³ hydrochloric acid at room temperature. The volume of hydrogen gas collected was measured at intervals:

| Time / s | 0 | 10 | 20 | 30 | 40 | 50 | 60 |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Volume of H₂ / cm³ | 0 | 18 | 30 | 38 | 42 | 44 | 44 |

(a) Calculate the average rate of reaction during the first 20 seconds.

(b) Calculate the average rate of reaction between \(t = 40\text{ s}\) and \(t = 60\text{ s}\).

(c) Explain, using collision theory, why the rate in (b) is lower than the rate in (a).

## Solution

**(a) Rate during the first 20 seconds.**

$$\text{average rate} = \frac{\Delta V}{\Delta t} = \frac{30 - 0}{20 - 0} = \frac{30}{20} = 1.5 \text{ cm}^3\text{s}^{-1}$$

**(b) Rate between \(t=40\text{ s}\) and \(t=60\text{ s}\).**

$$\text{average rate} = \frac{44 - 42}{60 - 40} = \frac{2}{20} = 0.1 \text{ cm}^3\text{s}^{-1}$$

**(c) Explanation using collision theory.**

Magnesium is in excess, so hydrochloric acid (HCl) is the reactant being used up as the reaction proceeds. As HCl is consumed, its concentration in the solution decreases, so there are fewer HCl particles per unit volume. This lowers the **frequency of collisions** between HCl particles and the magnesium surface, and therefore lowers the frequency of successful (effective) collisions per second. With fewer effective collisions per second, the rate of reaction — and hence the rate of gas production — decreases over time, as reflected by the smaller rate found in (b) compared with (a).

**Check:** the volume readings themselves confirm this trend even without computing exact rates — the gas volume increases by 18, 12, 8, 4, 2, 0 cm³ in each successive 10-second interval, a steadily *decreasing* set of increments, consistent with a steadily decreasing rate. The graph also correctly plateaus (no further gas produced after \(t=50\text{ s}\)) once the limiting reagent, HCl, is fully used up.

**Common mistake:** calculating the rate over (b) using the full time from \(t=0\) instead of the given interval \(t=40\) to \(t=60\text{ s}\) (i.e. using \(44/60\) instead of \((44-42)/(60-40)\)). This gives the *average rate over the whole experiment*, not the rate during that specific interval, and will not show that the reaction has slowed down.

## Answer

(a) \(1.5\text{ cm}^3\text{s}^{-1}\) (b) \(0.1\text{ cm}^3\text{s}^{-1}\) (c) Decreasing HCl concentration lowers collision frequency, so the rate falls over time.
