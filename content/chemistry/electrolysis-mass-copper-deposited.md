## Question

Aqueous copper(II) sulfate is electrolysed using copper electrodes. A steady current of 0.5 A is passed through the cell for 32 minutes 10 seconds.

[\(A_r\) of Cu = 64; 1 mole of electrons carries a charge of 96500 C; cathode reaction: \( \text{Cu}^{2+}(aq) + 2e^- \rightarrow \text{Cu}(s) \)]

Calculate the mass of copper deposited at the cathode.

## Solution

**Step 1 — convert time to seconds.**

$$t = 32 \times 60 + 10 = 1930 \text{ s}$$

**Step 2 — charge passed.**

$$Q = I \times t = 0.5 \times 1930 = 965 \text{ C}$$

**Step 3 — moles of electrons.**

$$n(e^-) = \frac{Q}{F} = \frac{965}{96500} = 0.0100 \text{ mol}$$

**Step 4 — moles of copper.** The cathode half-equation shows 2 mol of electrons deposit 1 mol of Cu, so:

$$n(\text{Cu}) = \frac{0.0100}{2} = 0.00500 \text{ mol}$$

**Step 5 — mass of copper.**

$$m = n \times A_r = 0.00500 \times 64 = 0.320 \text{ g}$$

**Check (working backwards):** 0.320 g Cu ÷ 64 = 0.00500 mol Cu → 0.0100 mol \(e^-\) → charge = \(0.0100 \times 96500 = 965\) C → time = \(965 \div 0.5 = 1930\) s, which matches the given time exactly.

**Common mistake:** forgetting that each Cu²⁺ ion needs **2** electrons, and dividing the charge by \(F\) to get moles of Cu directly (skipping the ÷2 step). This gives an answer of 0.640 g — exactly double the correct mass.

## Answer

Mass of copper deposited = **0.320 g** (3 s.f.)
