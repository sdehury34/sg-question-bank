## Question

Excess marble chips (calcium carbonate) are reacted with dilute hydrochloric acid:

$$\text{CaCO}_3(s) + 2\text{HCl}(aq) \rightarrow \text{CaCl}_2(aq) + \text{H}_2\text{O}(l) + \text{CO}_2(g)$$

The volume of \(\text{CO}_2\) gas collected is recorded every 20 seconds:

| Time / s | 0 | 20 | 40 | 60 | 80 | 100 | 120 |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Volume of CO₂ / cm³ | 0 | 24 | 42 | 54 | 60 | 63 | 63 |

(a) Calculate the average rate of reaction during the first 40 seconds, in cm³/s.

(b) State, with reference to collision theory, why the rate of reaction decreases as the experiment proceeds, even though the marble chips are in excess.

## Solution

**Part (a) — average rate.** Average rate of reaction is the change in volume of gas produced divided by the time taken:

$$\text{average rate} = \frac{\Delta V}{\Delta t} = \frac{V_{40} - V_{0}}{40 - 0} = \frac{42 - 0}{40} = 1.05\ \text{cm}^3/\text{s}$$

**Check:** the rate should be highest near the start of the reaction (steepest section of the volume-time curve) and this matches — between 100 s and 120 s the volume is constant at 63 cm³ (the acid has been used up, since the chips are in excess), so the reaction has stopped by then, confirming the graph shape is consistent with a reaction that starts fast and slows down.

**Part (b) — why rate decreases.** The marble chips are in excess, so it is the acid that is the limiting reagent and its concentration steadily decreases as it is used up. A lower concentration of HCl means fewer acid particles (H⁺ ions) per unit volume, so the frequency of effective collisions between acid particles and the surface of the marble chips decreases. By collision theory, a lower collision frequency means a lower rate of reaction — this is why the volume-time graph becomes progressively less steep and eventually flattens out once the acid is completely used up (at 63 cm³, no more gas is produced no matter how long you wait).

**Common mistake:** calculating the average rate over the *whole* experiment (63 cm³ ÷ 120 s = 0.525 cm³/s) instead of the specific 0–40 s interval asked for, or quoting the answer without units (cm³/s). Another common error in part (b) is saying the marble chips "run out" — here the chips are stated to be in excess, so it is the acid concentration that falls, not the amount of solid.

## Answer

(a) Average rate during the first 40 s = **1.05 cm³/s**

(b) The acid (limiting reagent) is used up, so its concentration falls, reducing the frequency of effective collisions between acid particles and the marble chips — hence the rate decreases and the reaction eventually stops at 63 cm³ of CO₂.
