## Question

In an experiment, 50 cm³ of 1.0 mol/dm³ hydrochloric acid is mixed with 50 cm³ of 1.0 mol/dm³ sodium hydroxide solution in an insulated cup. The temperature of the mixture rises from 25.0 °C to 31.7 °C.

Assume the resulting solution has density 1.0 g/cm³ and specific heat capacity 4.2 J/g/°C, and that no heat is lost to the surroundings.

Calculate the enthalpy change of neutralisation, in kJ/mol.

## Solution

**Step 1 — Find the heat released, \(q\).**

Total volume of solution = \(50 + 50 = 100\) cm³, so mass \(m = 100\) g (density 1.0 g/cm³).

$$q = mc\Delta T = 100 \times 4.2 \times (31.7 - 25.0) = 100 \times 4.2 \times 6.7 = 2814 \text{ J} = 2.814 \text{ kJ}$$

**Step 2 — Find the number of moles of water formed.**

$$n(\text{HCl}) = \frac{1.0}{1000} \times 50 = 0.050 \text{ mol}, \qquad n(\text{NaOH}) = \frac{1.0}{1000} \times 50 = 0.050 \text{ mol}$$

Since HCl and NaOH react in a 1:1 mole ratio (\( \text{HCl} + \text{NaOH} \rightarrow \text{NaCl} + \text{H}_2\text{O} \)) and both are present in equal moles, exactly 0.050 mol of water is formed — neither reagent is in excess.

**Step 3 — Find the enthalpy change per mole.**

$$\Delta H = -\frac{q}{n} = -\frac{2.814}{0.050} = -56.28 \text{ kJ/mol}$$

The value is negative because the temperature **rose**, meaning heat was released by the reaction (exothermic).

**Check.** Reversing the calculation: \( 0.050 \text{ mol} \times 56.28 \text{ kJ/mol} = 2.814 \text{ kJ} = 2814\text{ J}\), and \(2814 \text{ J} \div (100 \times 4.2) = 6.7\text{ °C}\), which matches the given temperature rise — confirming the arithmetic.

**Common mistake:** using only 50 g (one solution's mass) instead of the combined 100 g of mixed solution in \(q = mc\Delta T\), which halves \(q\) and gives an enthalpy change of roughly −28.1 kJ/mol instead of the correct −56.3 kJ/mol. Also, forgetting the negative sign for an exothermic (temperature-rising) reaction is a common error.

## Answer

The enthalpy change of neutralisation is **−56.3 kJ/mol** (3 s.f.).
